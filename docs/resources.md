## Part 1: Clinical Trial Findings — Affordable Prosthetic Devices

### Search Summary
ClinicalTrials.gov was searched for recent studies on affordable prosthetic devices. An aggregate analysis of **4,986 studies** was performed, and the most relevant individual studies are profiled below.

### Aggregate Analysis — Trial Phases
| Phase | Count | Interpretation |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| **NA (Device/Technology Evaluation)** | 2,839 | Most prosthetic research doesn't fit traditional drug-phase categories — these are device feasibility and technique studies |
| **Unknown** | 1,597 | Recruiting or status not yet posted; likely includes many ongoing affordable device trials |
| **Phase 3** | 149 | Late-stage trials — most promising for FDA-cleared affordable devices reaching market |
| **Phase 4** | 238 | Post-market studies — real-world effectiveness of already-approved affordable devices |
| **Phase 2** | 141 | Mid-stage — testing novel affordable prosthetic technologies with preliminary efficacy data |
| **Phase 1** | 53 | Early safety studies for new implantable prosthetic components |
| **Early Phase 1** | 14 | First-in-human exploratory studies |

**Key takeaway:** The large "NA" and "Unknown" categories reflect that prosthetic device research is typically evaluated on feasibility and functional outcomes rather than traditional phase gates. **Phase 3 (149 studies)** and **Phase 2 (141 studies)** are the most promising for commercially viable affordable devices.

### Most Promising Sponsors (Deep Dive)
1. **Shirley Ryan AbilityLab** (Chicago) — A world-class rehabilitation hospital directly tackling affordable socket fabrication for lower-limb amputees. Their completed study shows sockets can be fitted in under 2 hours vs. the traditional multi-visit process.
2. **Sint Maartenskliniek** (Netherlands) — Pioneered the $80 ReMotion Knee, demonstrating that reverse innovation from low-income countries can drastically cut costs in high-income settings.
3. **UHCW NHS Trust** (Coventry, UK) — Public NHS trust developing an affordable upper-limb prosthesis with sensory feedback, addressing the 50% of upper-limb amputees who abandon prescribed devices.
4. **Universite de Montreal** (Canada) — Studying patient-specific surgical guides that are far more affordable than robotic computer navigation while improving surgical precision.
5. **Hospital Evangelique de Curitiba** (Brazil) — Exploring 3D-printed PLA spacers with vancomycin for periprosthetic infection treatment at a fraction of current costs — Phase 1/2 is the most promising new implant approach.

### Key Findings for Amputees

#### Strengths
- **Exceptional public transit (9.3/10):** The Halsted CTA Orange Line station is only 380 meters away, providing direct access to downtown Chicago and connections to other healthcare facilities. This is critical for amputees who may not yet be able to drive.
- **Top-tier walkability (10/10):** The neighborhood flat terrain, sidewalk infrastructure, and accessible intersections make it ideal for prosthetic users and wheelchair users.
- **Rich food & services ecosystem:** 197 restaurants and 19 service providers within walking/transit distance reduce the need for long trips.
- **Fitness options (8.9/10):** 8 gyms/fitness centers nearby — important for amputee rehabilitation and prosthetic training.
- **Grocery access (8.3/10):** Multiple supermarkets with wheelchair-accessible entrances.

#### Gaps & Concerns
- **No healthcare facilities within 2km:** This is a significant concern. Amputees need frequent follow-up visits for prosthetic adjustments, skin checks, and rehabilitation. The nearest major hospitals would require transit trips. **Recommendation:** Map out nearby rehabilitation medicine clinics and prosthetics providers beyond the 2km radius.
- **No parks or green spaces:** Outdoor rehabilitation, stress reduction, and community gathering spaces are important for emotional and physical recovery. Amputees benefit from accessible outdoor areas for walking practice.
- **Limited specialist care:** While general services are plentiful, the area lacks dedicated prosthetics/orthotics labs, occupational therapy centers, or amputee support facilities within the immediate radius.

### Recommendations for the Amputee Community
1. **Use public transit for healthcare access:** The Halsted CTA station provides excellent connections to Northwestern Memorial, Henry Ford Hospital, and other major rehab centers across Chicago.
2. **Leverage local fitness centers:** Strength Club (446m) and Transformation Fitness offer adaptive fitness programs that can complement amputee rehabilitation.
3. **Plan for healthcare trips outside the neighborhood:** Identify prosthetists and rehab specialists at facilities like Shirley Ryan AbilityLab itself, which is a leading center for amputee prosthetic care.
4. **Advocate for green space:** The lack of parks within 2km is a community health gap — push for accessible outdoor rehabilitation spaces in the Bridgeport/South Loop area.
5. **Utilize the dense service network:** With 19 service providers (banks, post office, ATMs) within walking distance, daily errands are manageable without driving — important for those still building mobility with a new prosthesis.
