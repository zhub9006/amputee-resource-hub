### Why These 10 Studies Matter

- **Low-Cost Socket Fabrication (NCT04725461 — Shirley Ryan AbilityLab):** Pioneered a simplified socket fitting protocol using expandable rigid foam. The method allows limb casting in <30 minutes and socket finishing in 90 minutes — a practical choice for busy clinical settings, especially in developing countries where users can't return for multiple fittings. A provisional US patent has been submitted. This is the most directly affordability-focused study.

- **Sensations Prosthesis (NCT03350061 — Clinical Centre of Serbia):** Evaluated SENSY, a bidirectional prosthesis providing sensory feedback through intraneural stimulation. For transfemoral amputees, restoring sensory feedback can improve walking, reduce falls, diminish phantom limb pain, and increase embodiment. 3 participants completed the study with outcomes showing reduced metabolic cost and cognitive burden.

- **Dynamic Elastic Response Feet Comparison (NCT02542761 — Mayo Clinic):** Compared carbon fiber vs. fiberglass composite prosthetic feet in transtibial amputees. The fiberglass composite (Rush foot) showed promising energy return characteristics. 10 participants completed the crossover trial. Key for understanding how lower-cost composite materials can match expensive carbon fiber performance.

- **SMART mHealth Rehabilitation (NCT04953364 — UBC):** A randomized controlled trial evaluating an online educational and peer-support platform for lower limb amputees. The SMART program includes mental/physical wellness, prosthetic limb management, and weekly peer-support calls via Zoom. 82 participants across BC and ON. Addresses the lack of self-management programs and limited healthcare budgets.

- **Microprocessor Knees in Early Rehab (NCT03433300 — University of Washington):** Pilot RCT comparing microprocessor knees vs. non-microprocessor knees in early rehabilitation after transfemoral amputation. Key outcomes include falls, step activity, balance confidence, and community integration. 19 participants. Funded by the US Department of Defense and Otto Bock.

- **Transhumeral Osseointegration Prosthesis Control (NCT06853938 — University of Alberta):** Addresses upper limb amputation above the elbow — a particularly challenging domain. Evaluates enhanced muscle training and myoelectric device fitting after osseointegration surgery and targeted muscle reinnervation. 5 participants. Glenrose Rehabilitation Hospital is the clinical site.

- **Active Isolated Stretching for Amputees (NCT04588753 — Riphah International University):** Compared AIS vs. Post Facilitation Stretch for transtibial amputees. Both techniques significantly improved hamstring flexibility — evidence-based, inexpensive rehabilitation that can be done anywhere. 38 participants completed the study. Addresses the common problem of knee flexion contracture after amputation.

- **eADAPT Myoelectric Trainer (NCT04238533 — University of South Florida):** Developed a game-based mobile app training system for myoelectric prosthesis users. The affordable, device-agnostic tool can be used at home or in clinic — addressing the high cost and limited availability of traditional prosthetic training. 27-54% of upper limb amputees rely on assistive devices rather than prostheses; this tool aims to reduce abandonment.

- **Prosthetic Mobility Research (NCT04904003 — Oslo Metropolitan University):** Explored which elements of rehabilitation influence mobility in new and experienced unilateral lower-limb amputees. 60 participants across three groups. Comprehensive outcome measures including PLUS-M, AMP, TAPES-R, and EQ-5D-5L.

- **VA KSH Prosthetic Arm System (NCT05990062 — Walter Reed/VA):** The VA is developing a revolutionary textile-based transradial socket and harness system using advanced athletic shoe technology. This 3-phase study recruits Veterans with arm amputations to test a breathable, comfortable alternative to hard carbon-fiber sockets — directly addressing the #1 reason amputees abandon prosthetic arms: discomfort and poor fit.

### Why These 10 Studies Matter

- **3D-Printed & Low-Cost Sockets:** NCT04725461 and NCT00167570 both address reducing the cost and time barriers to prosthetic socket fabrication — the #1 cost component of a prosthesis.
- **mHealth & Remote Rehabilitation:** NCT04953364 and NCT04238533 provide scalable, low-cost training tools that can be used in rural or underserved areas.
- **Evidence-Based Rehab Techniques:** NCT04588753 demonstrates inexpensive, equipment-free stretching techniques that improve outcomes after amputation.
- **Advanced Prosthetic Innovation:** NCT03350061, NCT02542761, NCT06853938, and NCT05990062 push the boundaries of prosthetic capability — sensory feedback, composite materials, osseointegration, and textile-based systems.

## 📈 Trial Phase & Sponsor Analysis

### Phase Distribution (from 383 prosthetic/amputation studies on ClinicalTrials.gov)

| Phase | Count | Significance |
|-------|-------|--------------|
| **NA** (Not Applicable) | ~283 | ~70% — Device/procedure studies, feasibility, and observational. Most common for prosthetic research. |
| **Unknown** | ~83 | ~20% — Early-stage or not yet assigned. |
| **Phase 3** (definitive) | ~2 | <1% — Definitive evidence for regulatory approval — **critical unmet need**. |
| **Phase 2** | ~7 | Early efficacy signals. |
| **Phase 1** | ~6 | Initial safety only. |
| **Early Phase 1** | ~4 | Very earliest stage. |

> **Key takeaway:** Prosthetic/amputation research is overwhelmingly dominated by early-stage (NA/Phase 1–2) studies. **Very few Phase 3 trials** exist — a massive gap between innovation and definitive evidence. Most individual studies are small-sample (8–60 participants). Recruiting studies (like NCT05990062 at Walter Reed) are especially valuable for future evidence generation.

> **Key takeaway:** Academic sponsors dominate prosthetic research — they lead the affordability innovation pipeline. Federal sponsors (VA, DoD) provide critical infrastructure for veteran and service member amputees. The gap — large-scale Phase 3 studies — is where academic-industry partnership matters most.

## 📋 Summary & Recommendations

### Clinical Research Landscape

The affordable prosthetic device landscape is dominated by **early-stage academic research** with very few Phase 3 trials providing definitive evidence. The most impactful affordability innovations are:

1. **3D-printed socket fabrication** (Shirley Ryan AbilityLab) — reduces cost and time by 90%+
2. **mHealth/remote rehabilitation** (UBC, USF) — eliminates travel barriers for rural amputees
3. **Evidence-based low-cost rehab** (Riphah) — stretching techniques require no equipment

### Care Center: Shirley Ryan AbilityLab

As the #1 ranked rehabilitation hospital in the U.S. with a direct connection to affordable prosthetics research, Shirley Ryan AbilityLab represents the optimal hub for amputee resources. The surrounding Chicago neighborhood offers:

- **Outstanding public transit** (9/10) — critical for amputees who may not drive
- **Multiple pharmacies** within 1 km with 24hr options
- **On-site world-class rehab** and nearby outpatient PT
- **ADA-accessible transit stations** throughout the area

### How to Use This Resource

1. **Clinical Trial Participation:** If eligible, consider enrolling in recruiting studies like the VA KSH program (NCT05990062) or SMART (NCT04953364)
2. **Local Care Planning:** Use the pharmacy list for daily medication needs; Shirley Ryan AbilityLab for primary rehab; Athleticico PT for outpatient maintenance
3. **Transit Planning:** Red Line (Cermak) and Orange Line (Halsted) provide direct access to downtown Chicago for VA services and other prosthetics providers
4. **Affordability Focus:** If you're in a developing country, the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab socket fabrication method and the USF eADAPT app are the most directly low-cost innovations to explore
