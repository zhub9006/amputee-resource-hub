### ⭐ Key Finding: Keeogo Powered Exoskeleton (NCT03264235 — Shirley Ryan AbilityLab, Chicago)

The **Keeogo powered exoskeleton** study at Shirley Ryan AbilityLab in Chicago stands out as one of the most directly relevant trials for affordable prosthetic/mobility assistive devices:

- **Technology:** A computer-controlled lower extremity motorized orthosis (Keeogo) worn over the user's hips and legs. Sensors supply information about kinematics and kinetics, and the system recognizes the user's mobility intentions.
- **Affordability Angle:** The device is designed to be a portable, wearable assistive technology that can be used in daily life — reducing the need for expensive, full-weight exoskeleton systems. It waits for the user's lead and assists according to the activity, making it efficient and cost-effective.
- **Sponsor:** Shirley Ryan AbilityLab (academic medical center) + U.S. Department of Education (federal funding)
- **Study Location:** Shirley Ryan AbilityLab, Chicago, IL 60611
- **Target Population:** Patients in inpatient rehabilitation, <3 months post-CVA, age 18+
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2017–2023)
- **Primary Outcomes:** Change in Timed Stair Climb Test speed, 5-times Sit to Stand Test, GAITRite gait data
- **Key Innovation:** Does not initiate movement — waits for the user's lead. This means it's a "smart assistant" rather than a powered walker, reducing cost and complexity.

### Additional Relevant Prosthetic Study: Vacuum Socket System (NCT01839123 — Columbus, OH)

- **Sponsor:** Ohio Willow Wood (industry) + Ohio State University (academic collaborator)
- **Location:** Columbus, Ohio
- **Focus:** Comparing vacuum suspension sockets vs. conventional suction/pin sockets for lower limb amputees
- **Affordability Relevance:** Vacuum suspension preserves residual-limb skin health, reduces skin breakdown and socket replacement frequency — directly relevant to affordable limb loss management
- **Published Outcome:** Rink C et al. (2016) in *J Rehabil Res Dev*

### Analysis of Trial Phases & Sponsors

**Trial Phase Observations:**
- **Device Evaluation / NA-phase studies** dominate prosthetic research — unlike drug trials, prosthetic and orthopedic device research often operates without traditional Phase 1/2/3 labeling
- **One completed Phase 4 study** (Negative Pressure Therapy, Englewood NJ) represents the most advanced interventional trial relevant to surgical wound care for amputees
- **One Phase 2 Recruiting study** (Stem Cells, Louisville) represents the regenerative medicine frontier that may eventually benefit amputee rehabilitation
- **Multiple completed studies** provide valuable retrospective data that could inform future prospective prosthetics research
- **AR feasibility studies** are emerging — representing the cutting edge of prosthetic rehabilitation technology

**Sponsor Analysis:**

- **Industry Sponsors** (Ohio Willow Wood, Integrum) — Industry involvement suggests translational potential; these companies have established distribution networks that could make innovations more accessible and affordable
- **Collaborative Academic-Industry Models** (OSU + Ohio Willow Wood; MIT + Brigham & Women's + Integrum) — The most promising pattern: university innovation paired with clinical validation and industry manufacturing capability
- **Federal Funding** (U.S. Department of Education) — Government support indicates potential for broader accessibility and affordability mandates
