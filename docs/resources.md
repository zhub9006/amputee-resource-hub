### Study Spotlights: Why These Matter for Amputees

**1. NCT04725461 — Low Cost Socket (Shirley Ryan AbilityLab)**
- The affordability story: An expandable rigid-foam socket fabricated in <90 minutes for pennies vs. thousands. Cast the limb in 30 min, finish the socket in 90 min using 3D-printed cylinder + expanding polyurethane foam. Patent pending. Validated in 8 participants at SRA.
- PIs: Laura Miller, Principal Investigator
- Key outcomes: Socket Comfort Score, Four Square Step Test, L-Test, 2-Min Walk Test
- **Why it matters:** Could enable prosthetic access in developing countries where socket fabrication requires multiple days and specialized prosthetists.

**2. NCT07645729 — Powered Ankle Prosthesis (MIT)**
- The frontier: Bidirectional neural control via osseointegrated implanted electrodes + cutaneous stimulation. Gait symmetry, embodiment, walking tasks. FDA-regulated device study.
- Eligibility: Unilateral transtibial amputation, K3+, ages 18-70. Cambridge, MA.

**3. NCT06719245 — PreventPAP (Netherlands)**
- The pain prevention angle: TMR during amputation to prevent phantom/residual limb pain. n=203 across 7 Dutch hospitals. Sham-controlled, randomized. ZonMw-funded.
- Why it matters: 61% of amputees develop post-amputation pain. Simple nerve handling vs. TMR. Results will influence global surgical standards.

**4. NCT05768802 — MIRA (Pittsburgh)**
- The implantable electrode promise: 8-channel intramuscular EMG array implanted under the skin. No percutaneous connections = lower infection risk. 1-year in-home trial. Ripple Therapeutics + NIH/NINDS.
- Eligibility: Transradial amputation. Device implanted for 1 year then explanted. Pittsburgh.

**5. NCT05296265 — VR for Phantom Pain (Albert Einstein)**
- The non-invasive approach: Active VR (games requiring leg movements) vs. Distractor VR (passive). n=46. Significant reductions in pain intensity, interference, depression, and anxiety. Philadelphia.
- **Why recruit:** If you suffer chronic phantom limb pain, VR therapy is accessible and requires no surgery.

### Disability-Specific Accessibility Notes

1. **ADA Compliance:** Most businesses in the area are ADA-compliant. Sidewalk curb cuts are present on major corridors.
2. **Wheelchair-Accessible Dining:** Multiple restaurants on Halsted and Archer offer step-free entry. Divvy adaptive bikes available.
3. **Paratransit:** CTA's paratransit service (Open Door) serves the Bridgeport area for those who cannot use fixed-route transit. Application required.
4. **Prosthetic Supply Access:** The 24/7 Walgreens at 757 N Michigan Ave is the closest emergency pharmacy, accessible via CTA Red Line or a short walk from SRA.
5. **SRA On-Site Services:** SRA provides on-site physical therapy, occupational therapy, prosthetic training, and peer mentoring — eliminating the need for external travel for initial rehab sessions.
6. **Accessible Transit:** CTA Red Line stations within 1 km have elevators, tactile platforms, and accessible fare gates. Bus stops have wheelchair ramps and priority seating.
