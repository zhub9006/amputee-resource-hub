### Key Trend Analysis

#### By Trial Phase (393 prosthetic/amputation studies)

| Phase | Count | % | Interpretation |
|-------|-------|---|----------------|
| **N/A** | 292 | 74.3% | Most studies are device/procedure evaluations without traditional drug-trial phases |
| Unknown | 82 | 20.9% | Lack of phase classification — often observational or early-stage |
| Phase 1 | 7 | 1.8% | Early safety/device testing |
| Phase 2 | 8 | 2.0% | Preliminary efficacy studies |
| Phase 3 | 3 | 0.8% | Large-scale randomized trials — very few reach this stage |

**Takeaway:** The overwhelming majority (74%) of prosthetic studies are non-phased, meaning they are device feasibility or observational studies. Very few progress to Phase 3, indicating a pipeline gap where promising low-cost devices rarely advance to large-scale clinical validation. This is especially true for affordable/low-cost device studies, which tend to remain in small-scale feasibility phases.

#### By Sponsor Type (393 studies)

| Sponsor Type | Count | % | Interpretation |
|-------------|-------|---|----------------|
| **Academic/Other (OTHER)** | 256 | 65.1% | Dominant — universities and research institutes drive most prosthetic research |
| **Industry (INDUSTRY)** | 82 | 20.9% | Second largest — companies developing commercial prosthetics |
| Federal (FED) | 45 | 11.5% | VA, DoD, NIH funding — critical for veteran/worker amputee research |
| Other Gov't | 8 | 2.0% | International/national health agencies |
| Network | 1 | 0.3% | Multi-site collaborative networks |

**Takeaway:** Academic institutions lead the field (65%), which explains why many studies are small-scale and feasibility-focused. Industry sponsorship is lower (21%) for low-cost/problem-specific devices, suggesting market gaps where affordable prosthetic innovation needs more public and philanthropic investment. Federal funding (11.5%) is crucial for veteran and traumatic amputation research.

- **Peer Support:** The Amputee Coalition of America and local Chicago amputee support groups meet at various locations; check with Shirley Ryan's rehabilitation counseling for schedules.

## 🔬 Implications for the Amputee Resource Hub

### What the Data Tells Us

1. **Affordable prosthetics are mostly in early/feasibility stages** — 74% of studies are non-phased, meaning most low-cost devices haven't reached large-scale validation. The Hub should highlight studies that show promise (ReMotion Knee, low-cost foam socket, DSR Ankle) and help users understand evidence levels.

2. **Academic research dominates** — 65% of studies come from universities and research institutes. The Hub should connect users with academic centers like Shirley Ryan AbilityLab for cutting-edge (possibly subsidized) prosthetic care and clinical trial opportunities.

3. **Geographic disparity** — The US has disproportionate research volume compared to the rest of the world. The Hub should provide resources for underserved populations, including telehealth options (validated by the Colombian TEFICA study).

4. **Neighborhood livability matters** — The Shirley Ryan AbilityLab area scores well for walkability (10/10) and public transit (9/10), which is critical for amputees who need frequent rehabilitation visits. However, healthcare proximity scores are moderate (4.9/10), and there are limited dedicated prosthetic supply shops nearby. The Hub should include a directory of local prosthetic providers and FAQs about transit access.

5. **Reverse innovation is promising** — The ReMotion Knee ($80 vs. $1,000-$5,000 standard) and low-cost foam socket methods represent real cost breakthroughs. The Hub should track which of these innovations are moving toward commercial availability.
