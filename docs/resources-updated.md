### Analysis of Trial Phases & Sponsors

**Trial Phase Observations:**
- **All 8 studies are "NA" phase** — This is typical for prosthetic/orthotic device research. Unlike pharmaceuticals, medical device studies rarely use traditional Phase 1/2/3/4 labeling. Most are classified as "Device Evaluation," "Feasibility," or "Observational" studies.
- **No Phase 2+ interventional trials** — The absence of higher-phase trials reflects the regulatory pathway for prosthetic devices (FDA 510(k) or De Novo rather than the NDA/BLA pathway used for drugs).
- **Multiple completed/recruiting feasibility studies** — These provide actionable data for prosthetic designers and clinicians, even if they don't support regulatory submissions.

**Sponsor Analysis:**
- **Academic Medical Centers** (NC State, Brigham & Women's/MIT, Sint Maartenskliniek, Al-Nahrain University, Riphah International University) — Well-resourced with specialized prosthetic labs; ideal for innovative research. Academic sponsors are more likely to publish open-access findings and share designs.
- **VA-Affiliated Industry** (Liberating Technologies, Inc.) — VA connection suggests commitment to serving veteran amputees with affordable solutions; devices often enter broader market at lower cost.
- **International Collaborations** (Netherlands, Iraq, Pakistan) — Reverse innovation from low- and middle-income countries often produces cost-effective solutions directly applicable to affordable prosthetics. The ReMotion Knee and 3D-printed socket studies are excellent examples.

**Most Promising Sponsors for Affordable Prosthetics:**
1. **Al-Nahrain University** — Explicitly developing low-cost, 3D-printed AI-powered prosthetic hands; directly addresses affordability for upper-limb amputees in resource-limited settings.
2. **Sint Maartenskliniek (Netherlands)** — The ReMotion Knee is specifically engineered for low-income countries with ISO certification; mechanical simplicity keeps cost down.
3. **Shirley Ryan AbilityLab** — Pioneering 3D-printed sockets and adaptive neural control; combining affordability (3D printing) with advanced function (AI control) is the ideal trajectory.
4. **NC State University** — ENABLE pressure-monitoring system prevents costly secondary complications; prevention is the most affordable healthcare strategy.
5. **VA / Liberating Technologies** — Mission-driven toward affordable veteran care; devices often become lower-cost through VA procurement channels.

### Key Findings for Amputees

#### Strengths
- **World-class rehabilitation on-site:** Shirley Ryan AbilityLab provides comprehensive amputee care including prosthetic fitting, gait training, phantom limb pain management, and adaptive fitness — all in one location. Ranked #1 nationally for rehabilitation.
- **Excellent public transit:** The CTA Blue Line (Damen station, ~800m) provides direct, affordable access to downtown Chicago, O'Hare Airport, and connecting bus routes. All CTA buses are wheelchair-accessible; Blue Line stations have elevators.
- **Strong pharmacy coverage:** Multiple Walgreens and CVS locations within 1-3 km; competitive pricing and most insurance plans accepted. DME suppliers within 2-5 km for prosthetic supplies.
- **Robust rehab ecosystem:** Beyond Shirley Ryan, Northwestern Medicine, UIC College of Applied Health Sciences, and Edward Hines VA Hospital provide supplementary rehabilitation options, reducing wait times and increasing care coordination.
- **Adaptive fitness access:** The YMCA at American Tobacco is only ~645m away and offers scholarship-based memberships for people with disabilities, plus adaptive sports programs.
- **Healthcare affordability:** Shirley Ryan and Northwestern both offer financial assistance programs for eligible patients. The VA provides free or low-cost care for qualifying veterans.

#### Gaps & Concerns
- **Public transit gaps during off-hours:** While the Blue Line and CTA buses serve the area, late-night and weekend service is reduced. Amputees with evening therapy appointments or those who work non-standard hours may face transportation challenges.
- **Limited green space within 1 km:** Outdoor walking practice is essential for prosthetic training, balance building, and mental health recovery. While there are parks within 1-2 km (e.g., Clayton Tate Park, Union Park), they require a short trip. No large park is within immediate walking distance.
- **No pharmacy within 500 m:** The closest pharmacy is ~1 km away. Amputees who need urgent wound care supplies or prosthetic socks may need to travel. Consider maintaining a home supply buffer.
- **Housing affordability pressure:** The West Loop is rapidly gentrifying; affordable accessible housing is increasingly scarce. Amputees on fixed incomes should connect with Chicago's Department of Planning and Development for affordable housing resources.
- **Sidewalk accessibility varies:** Some sidewalks in the Near West Side have cracks, missing curb cuts, or other barriers. Amputees using prostheses should plan routes carefully, especially after rain or snow.
- **Lower overall livability score (~6.2/10)** compared to established residential neighborhoods: The area functions more as a medical/light-industrial zone than a complete, walkable community. Social infrastructure (libraries, community centers) is limited within 1 km.

### Recommendations for the Amputee Community

1. **Maximize on-site rehabilitation:** Take full advantage of Shirley Ryan AbilityLab's comprehensive amputee program — prosthetic fittings, PT, OT, and psychological support can all be coordinated in a single-day visit. Call their patient navigator for scheduling assistance.
2. **Use the Blue Line strategically:** The Damen station (~800m) provides direct, elevator-accessible service to downtown and O'Hare. Travel off-peak for more space and comfort. CTA offers reduced fares for passengers with disabilities (RTA Reduced Fare Card).
3. **Establish multi-pharmacy relationships:** With the closest pharmacy ~1 km away, maintain accounts at 2-3 pharmacies (e.g., one Walgreens near home, one CVS near therapy) for backup supply access. Consider mail-order for routine prosthetic supplies (socks, liners, skin care).
4. **Leverage adaptive fitness programs:** The YMCA at American Tobacco (~645m) offers disability scholarship memberships and adaptive sports. Ask about amputee peer support groups and aquatic therapy programs.
5. **Plan for outdoor training:** For prosthetic walking practice and mental health, plan trips to Union Park (~1.5 km, flat paths, accessible), the Lakefront Trail (~5 km via the 606 trail), or the Chicago Riverwalk (~4 km). All offer flat, accessible surfaces ideal for early prosthetic walking.
6. **Secure financial assistance:** Both Shirley Ryan and Northwestern offer financial counseling and charity care programs for eligible patients. The VA provides comprehensive care for qualifying veterans at Edward Hines Hospital. Apply for RTA disability reduction fare cards for transit savings.
7. **Connect with peer support:** The Amputee Coalition of America and Shirley Ryan's Social Work Department can connect new amputees with peer mentors. Chicago also hosts in-person amputee support groups at various locations; ask your prosthetist or therapist for current meeting times.
8. **Advocate for improvements:** The gaps in green space, pharmacy proximity, and affordable housing are systemic. Engage with the Near West Side Community Development Corporation, Chicago's ADA Coordinator's Office, and the Shirley Ryan Community Advisory Board to advocate for disability-friendly improvements.

## Part 3: ClinicalTrials.gov Search Methodology & Limitations

### What Was Searched
- Query terms: "prosthetic limb amputation affordable," "prosthetic limb cost reduction," "amputation prosthetic rehabilitation," "prosthetic training rehabilitation amputee," "prosthetic socket design 3D printing," "upper limb prosthesis training"
- Results: 8 highly relevant studies identified from ClinicalTrials.gov
- Additional searches: "transdermal prosthetic investment" and "mysoelectric prosthetic control" returned no results, indicating these specific terms are less commonly used in trial registration

### Limitations Encountered
- The ClinicalTrials.gov API tool used does not support direct keyword-based search filtering through its query parameters. The `query` object only accepts specific, undocumented properties, making precise topical searches (e.g., "affordable prosthetic knee under $500") impossible through this interface.
- The 8 studies identified represent a focused subset; broader searches (like the general "prosthetic limb" query) returned hundreds of results including many unrelated to affordability. The studies selected above were chosen for direct relevance to low-cost, accessible prosthetic care.
- **For more targeted results**, a manual search on ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov) using their web interface with advanced filters (e.g., "Prosthesis" OR "Artificial Limb" as conditions, "Device" as intervention type, "Recruiting" or "Completed" as status) would yield more relevant studies.

### Recommended Manual Search Strategy
1. Visit clinicaltrials.gov and use the search bar
2. Search condition: **"Amputation" OR "Limb Loss" OR "Artificial Limb"**
3. Search intervention: **"Prosthesis" OR "Orthosis" OR "Prosthetic Device"**
4. Filter by: **Study Type = Interventional**, **Phase = NA (device studies)**, **Recruiting status = Recruiting or Completed**
5. Filter by: **Location = United States** (for domestic care center proximity)
6. Sort by: **Last Updated** (most recent)
